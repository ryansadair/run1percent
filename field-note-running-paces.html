Tempo, Threshold, Marathon Pace & Everything In Between

November 2025 10 min read

Serious distance runners live or die by their ability to understand and apply the right intensity at the right time. Misjudge a workout and you waste weeks. Nail these distinctions and every session compounds.

Core Philosophy

Paces are not rigid numbers — they are physiological tools. The best runners use them with intention. Context (fatigue, heat, hills, recovery) always matters more than any single percentage.

The Pace Hierarchy

1. Easy / Recovery Runs

  • Purpose: Aerobic base, capillary development, fat metabolism, recovery
  • How it feels: Conversational. You could do this all day.
  • RPE: 3–5 / 10
  • Typical duration: 45–120+ minutes
This is where 70–80% of your weekly mileage should live. Protect your easy days fiercely — they are not “junk miles.”

2. Long Run / Aerobic Endurance Pace

Comfortably challenging but sustainable. Speak in short sentences. RPE 5–6.5. Usually 90 minutes to 3+ hours. Finish the last 20–30% strong when possible.

3. Marathon Pace (MP)

Hard but controlled. The effort you could sustain for the full race distance on a good day. RPE: 7–8 / 10. Highly individual and improves dramatically with strong aerobic development.

4. Tempo / Threshold Runs

The most commonly confused paces

Lactate Threshold (LT) is the highest intensity you can sustain for roughly 45–60 minutes before lactate builds rapidly.

Tempo Run: 20–40 minutes (continuous or broken) at or very near LT pace. “Comfortably hard.” You can say a few words but not hold a conversation. RPE 8–8.5.

5. VO2 Max Intervals

3K–5K race pace (or fastest effort you could hold for ~8–12 minutes). Very hard breathing. RPE 9–9.5. Reps of 3–8 minutes with equal or slightly shorter recovery.

6. Repetition / Speed Work

Mile pace or faster. Short, sharp efforts (200m–800m) with full recovery. RPE 9.5–10. Used sparingly for neuromuscular power and economy.

Practical Application Tips

  • 01. 80/20 Rule: 80% easy/long running, 20% quality work above tempo.
  • 02. Easy days must stay truly easy. The magic happens when hard sessions are truly hard.
  • 03. Use perceived effort and breathing as your primary guide. Data supports, it doesn’t dictate.

For those still working toward their first serious half or marathon: Don’t obsess over perfect paces yet. Master the difference between “easy” and “not easy.” Everything else becomes dramatically easier with time.

The 1% difference isn’t knowing these terms — it’s consistently applying them with intention, week after week.
