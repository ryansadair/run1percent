One Watch Metric That Actually Matters

January 2025 8 min read

There are dozens of metrics on modern running watches: VO2 max estimates, recovery scores, training load, aerobic/anaerobic training effect, etc. Most of them are interesting. Very few of them actually move the needle for serious half marathon and marathon performance. After years of testing every new feature that comes out, there’s one metric I keep coming back to as disproportionately valuable: Running Power (or more specifically, your ability to hold normalized power sustainably). But let’s be real—most runners obsess over the wrong numbers. Pace lies in the heat or on hills. Heart rate drifts with fatigue, dehydration, or heat. Even “fitness” scores can be misleading. Why Power Cuts Through the Noise It gives you a relatively consistent external measure of work output that isn’t as heavily influenced by environmental conditions or daily physiological fluctuations. When used correctly, it becomes an incredibly honest feedback tool during both training and racing. How Serious Runners Actually Use It Training: Learn your power zones the way you once learned heart rate zones. Do key workouts by power (especially tempo and threshold work). Racing: Use power as a ceiling in the early miles when excitement tries to override strategy. Progress tracking: Watch how your power at marathon pace evolves over a training block. That trend tells you more about real fitness gains than most other metrics combined. Important nuance for aspiring runners If you’re still building your base and working toward your first half marathon, don’t get lost in power meter rabbit holes yet. Master consistent mileage, easy running, and basic pacing first. Power becomes exponentially more useful once you have a solid aerobic foundation. The best runners I know don’t chase every shiny metric. They pick one or two that give them reliable, actionable truth and ignore the rest until they actually need them. In a world drowning in data, the ability to focus on what actually matters is itself a 1% skill.
